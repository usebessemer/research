# The space-by-time economy of the context window

## TL;DR

A model has a bounded context window: a finite set of slots, filled at a moment, that the model attends to all at once. Everything placed before the model, files, agents, tools, memories, connections, skills, competes for that finite attention. This paper argues for one universal economy and one precise condition on its sharpest form. **The economy is universal:** anything placed before a finite, all-at-once, per-call window is economized by deciding **where** it is present (scope) and **when**, and for how long, it is present (time). Space and time, every time. But the economy factors into **two independent, peer axes**, a clean grid with structural theorems like the empty cell, **only when the element carries an intrinsic second dimension for the time-lever to bind to.** Files carry **content-type**; agents carry **lifecycle**. With such a dimension, time is a genuine peer axis, free of scope and varying independently of it. Without it, the time-lever does not vanish, but it collapses into scope: deciding when becomes the same act as deciding where, and no clean two-axis grid forms. The paper shows this across three populations. Files factor cleanly *because* they carry content-type (this is the keystone, and it corrects v1, which wrongly waved content-type away). Agents factor cleanly *because* they carry lifecycle (with an honest account of the asymmetry: the routing hierarchy is genuinely shared, but the agent's first axis, Position, is a functional role that is scope-*like* rather than a literal partition of situation-space). Tools are the worked **bounding case**: they obey the scope economy cleanly, but they carry no intrinsic second dimension, so their time-lever collapses into scope, no empty-cell theorem reproduces, and they sit at "one-and-a-half axes." The bounding case proves the rule the way the topology's empty cell does. The honest status is declared up front: the universal economy is **argued** (the framework half), and so is exhaustiveness, which rests on a "situation"-shaped soft joint named openly; the generalization to all future populations is a **conjecture** offered for refutation, with strengthened falsifiers that cannot be redescribed away.

## Why a root

Two papers in this repository reach for the same shape from different directions. *Context as Architecture* takes an informal "put files where they belong" intuition and resolves it into independent axes with a classification function, which is what makes a file tree machine-checkable. *The agent-role topology* takes an informal "one lead plus leaves" intuition and resolves it into orthogonal axes with a grid, which is what makes a role structure machine-checkable. Each paper notices, in its own register, that scope and time keep coming up as the organizing pair. The topology paper closes by naming the coincidence explicitly and flagging it as an open conjecture: a "space-by-time economy" forced by the bounded context window, predicted to recur in any layer built against a finite window, "a fuller treatment is left to a future foundational piece."

This is that piece. Its job is not to re-derive either companion paper but to sit *underneath* both: to state the principle they are each an instance of, to argue it rather than assert it, and to turn the closing conjecture from a one-paragraph aside into a falsifiable claim with stated refutation conditions. But this piece also does something the conjecture did not anticipate, and it is the more interesting result. An adversarial pass on the strong form of the conjecture (that *every* population factors cleanly into exactly two peer axes) broke it. A third population, tools, does not factor that way, and looking harder at the first two showed *why*: the clean two-axis form is not guaranteed by the window: it is purchased by a property of the population. So the refined thesis is not weaker than the conjecture; it is sharper. It states both the universal economy and the precise condition under which that economy takes its cleanest, grid-with-theorems form. The two existing papers become the first two corollaries; tools become the boundary that locates the condition.

The discipline is the one both companion papers used on their own subjects: take a thing everyone half-knows and resolve it until a checker, or a critic, could disagree with it precisely. The thing everyone half-knows here is "context is scarce, so be selective." The resolution is: scarcity of an all-at-once resource is always economized by where and when, and the population gets a clean two-axis grid exactly when it carries an intrinsic second dimension for the when-lever to bind to.

## The principle

Start with what a context window *is*, because the whole argument rests on it.

A context window is the text a model considers in a single inference call, measured in tokens. Two properties of it matter, and only these two:

1. **It is bounded.** The window holds a finite number of tokens. Past the bound, content is not admitted; under degradation effects (the "lost in the middle" finding), even content that *is* admitted is attended to unevenly, so the *effective* budget is tighter than the nominal one. Either way, the resource is finite. There is a ceiling on what can be present at once, and a softer ceiling, below it, on what can be present *and well-attended* at once.

2. **It is filled at a moment, and attended all at once.** The window is not a stream the model reads left to right and forgets; it is a set of slots, assembled for one call, all of which the model attends to simultaneously in that call. Inference is a function of the whole filled window. There is no "later in the call" in which something not loaded now becomes available; the window for *this* call is fixed when the call is made, and the next call is a fresh fill.

Put those two together and you have a finite resource that is *re-provisioned per call*. That is the exact profile of a classic scarce resource under a renewable budget: at each moment you have a fixed capacity, and you must decide what occupies it *this* time. Cache lines, a working set of memory pages, a register file, a single screen of a progressively-disclosed UI: each is a fixed-capacity slot-set, re-filled per cycle, where the only question is *what is present now*. The context window is one more member of that family, and the economics of that family are well understood.

Here is the economic claim, stated plainly. **For a finite resource that is provisioned at a moment and attended all at once, economizing it means deciding, for each candidate element, whether it occupies a slot in this provisioning.** The decision for one element has two dimensions:

- **Where (scope).** Of all the contexts the system can be in, in *which* of them is this element relevant enough to occupy a slot? Scope routes an element to the situations it belongs in and away from the situations it does not. It is a *positional* question: it partitions the space of situations and admits the element only in its partition.

- **When (time).** Given the situations where the element is in-scope, *at which moments* within them, and for *how long*, should it actually occupy a slot? Time schedules an element's presence: always (it occupies a slot in every provisioning while in scope), on demand (it occupies a slot only when a triggering condition holds), or per item (it occupies a slot for exactly one work-item's duration and then yields it). It is a *temporal* question: it gates presence by moment and bounds it by duration.

Scope decides the *set of situations* an element may appear in. Time decides *when, and for how long*, within those situations, it actually does. The first is a partition of space; the second is a schedule over time. Every act of economizing the window is some setting of these two: present this element *here* (scope) and *then, for that long* (time). **That is the universal part of the principle, and it holds for every population this paper examines, including the bounding one.** What does *not* hold universally is that these two are always cleanly *separable* into peer axes; that is the refined thesis, and it is the subject of the next two sections. The rest of the paper argues the universal economy, argues the condition for the clean two-axis form, shows two populations meeting the condition and one failing it, and turns the whole into a prediction.

## The two levers, and when they are two peer axes

The principle would be empty if "where and when" were just a capacious phrase. The objection is fair and must be met directly, not waved past: *of course* you can describe any organizing decision as being about where and when something happens, in the same way you can describe any event as having a location and a time. That framing absorbs everything and therefore explains nothing. If that were all this claim amounted to, it would be a tautology wearing the costume of a result.

The claim is stronger and more falsifiable than that, and the strength lives in two properties the two levers must *earn*, not merely be named with: **independence** and **exhaustiveness**. The non-trivial content is entirely in these two. A tautology has neither. And, crucially, the refined thesis is that **independence is not automatic.** It is the property a population has to *carry the means to satisfy*. The universal economy gives you two *levers*. Whether those two levers are two *peer axes* you can set separately depends on whether the element has an intrinsic second dimension for the time-lever to bind to. This is the heart of the paper, so the next two subsections are careful.

### Independence is earned, and an enabling second dimension is how

Two axes are independent if fixing one leaves the other free: knowing an element's value on one axis tells you nothing about its value on the other. For scope and time to be independent, it must be possible to vary an element's *when* while holding its *where* fixed, and vice versa.

Hold scope fixed. Take a set of elements all in-scope for the *same* situation, say, everything relevant to one client engagement. Within that fixed scope, do they still differ in their time setting? For files, yes, demonstrably: the engagement's identity file is *always* present while you work the engagement; its reference SOP is present *on demand* when a writing task calls for it; the specific stage file is present *per item* for one stage and then yields. Same scope, three different time settings. So for files, fixing scope does not fix time.

Now hold time fixed. Take a set of files all on the *same* time setting, say, everything that is *always loaded* while in scope. They still differ in scope: a global identity statement is always-loaded *everywhere*; a workspace-specific situational fact is always-loaded *only within that workspace*. Same time setting, two different scopes. So for files, fixing time does not fix scope.

But notice *what made that possible* for files, because it is the load-bearing observation of this whole paper. A file's "always vs on-demand vs per-item" setting could vary independently of its scope because a file has a property *other than its scope and other than its load-timing* that the load-timing tracks: **content-type**. An identity file is always-loaded not as a free choice but because it is *identity*; a reference is on-demand because it is *reference*; a working file is per-item because it is a *work product*. The load pattern (time) is keyed to content-type, and content-type is orthogonal to scope. So time can run free of scope *because there is a third property of the file, intrinsic to it, for the time-lever to attach to and ride independently.* Remove that property, force the time setting to be readable only off "where the element is and whether this is the call that needs it," and the time decision and the scope decision become the same decision. That is exactly what happens to tools, below.

A sharper way to see independence when it holds: scope is a question about the *partition of situations*, and time is a question about the *schedule within a situation*. These are different mathematical objects. A partition assigns each element to a region of situation-space. A schedule assigns, within a region, a presence pattern over moments and a duration. You cannot in general derive a partition from a schedule or a schedule from a partition; they answer different questions about different domains (space of situations versus line of moments). When a population carries an intrinsic second dimension keying the schedule, the two objects are genuinely separable and the independence is structural. When it does not, the only thing the schedule can key on is the situation itself, and the schedule degenerates into a re-description of the partition. **Independence is structural when earned and absent when not; the enabling dimension is what earns it.**

### Exhaustiveness, argued not proven, with the soft joint named

Exhaustiveness is the other half: any economization of the window is expressible as a setting of scope and time, with no residue. There is no third *kind of thing* you can do to a finite, all-at-once, per-call resource that is not reducible to *where it is present* and *when, and for how long*, it is present.

The argument is by elimination, and it leans on the two structural properties of the window. What can you actually *do* to economize a slot-set that is filled at a moment and attended all at once? You can only control **which elements occupy slots in a given provisioning**, because that is the only thing the window exposes: a set of occupied-or-not slots at call time. So the entire space of economizing actions is: *for each element, in which provisionings does it occupy a slot?* A provisioning is identified by the **situation** the system is in (which determines the *set* of candidate elements that could be relevant) and the **moment** the call happens (which determines *which* of the in-scope candidates are actually triggered, and for how long they stay). "Which provisionings does this element occupy a slot in?" therefore decomposes into "which *situations*?" (scope) and "at which *moments within them, for how long*?" (time). Priority, relevance score, recency, frequency, cost, all the things one might reach for as a "third axis", are not additional coordinates; they are *inputs to the scope and time decisions*. A relevance score is how you *decide* a thing's scope or trigger its time setting; it is not a separate place a thing can be. Compression and summarization, likewise, change *how much* of an element occupies its slot, not whether-and-when it does; they operate *within* a time setting, not beside it.

Here is where v1 overclaimed, and where this version is honest. v1 stated this elimination with theorem-like confidence: "the levers, full stop, no residue." That confidence was the overclaim, and an adversarial pass finds the soft joint. The elimination's strength is exactly the strength of one premise: **"a provisioning is a (situation, moment) pair and nothing else."** That premise has a sink in it. "Situation" is elastic. Almost any candidate third coordinate of a provisioning, the user's role this call, the channel it came in on, the trust level of the requester, the budget remaining, can be absorbed into "the situation the system is in" and thereby re-described as scope, rather than standing as a genuine third coordinate. So the elimination does not so much *prove* there is no third axis as *define* the situation broadly enough that nothing escapes it. That is an argument, and a good one, but it is not a theorem, because its key term is doing load-bearing work by being elastic.

The honest move is to *name the joint and offer a criterion*, even an imperfect one, that distinguishes a genuine third coordinate from a re-description. **Criterion (offered, not proven decisive): a candidate property is a coordinate of a provisioning if the element's slot-occupancy must condition on it *and it cannot be folded into the partition of situations without making the partition track the element's own time-varying state*.** Put plainly: if the property is a stable feature of the world the call happens in, it is part of the situation, and it is scope. If the property is a feature of the *element's own changing condition* that the schedule reads, it is time. If it is genuinely neither, a coordinate of the call that is not a fact about the world's situation and not a fact about the element's schedule, *that* would be a real third axis, and the framework predicts you will not find one for a bounded, per-call, all-at-once window. But the criterion has a fuzzy edge precisely where "situation" is elastic, and we flag it as a soft joint rather than pretending the elimination closes it. **Exhaustiveness is therefore argued, not proven, and it wears that status as visibly as the generalization conjecture does.** A reader who can exhibit a property that the criterion cannot cleanly file as scope, time, or input has found the joint's give, and that is a falsifier (below), not a footnote.

What survives, and survives strongly, is the *minimum*: a single axis cannot economize the window for any population whose where and when can come apart, because then a one-axis scheme must either fix scope and vary only time (and cannot express "this element belongs only in some situations") or fix time and vary only scope (and cannot express "this in-scope element should be present only on demand"). Both failures have names in the file paper: monolithic-context is the degenerate case where everything is always-present (time collapsed); hidden-context is the degenerate case where something has a notional scope but no time setting ever loads it. So **two is the minimum that economizes any population whose levers separate, and, by the argued elimination, the maximum that has a coordinate to economize.** The clean *count of two peer axes*, though, is conditional, which is the next section's whole point.

## The condition for the clean two-axis form

Pull the two subsections together into the thesis, because it is the spine of the paper.

The where/when economy is **universal**: every element before a finite, all-at-once, per-call window is economized by deciding where it is present and when, for how long. That never fails. What is **conditional** is whether the economy *factors* into two independent peer axes, a clean grid you can read off one coordinate at a time, with structural theorems (like the empty cell) that only a genuine grid can host.

**The condition: the economy factors into two independent peer axes exactly when the element carries an intrinsic second dimension for the time-lever to bind to, independent of scope.** Files carry content-type. Agents carry lifecycle. With such a dimension, the time setting is keyed to a property other than scope, so time varies freely at fixed scope and the two are genuine peers. Without it, the time-lever has nothing to key on but the situation, so "when is this present" collapses into "in which situations is this present", and you have one axis (scope) with a time-flavored gloss, not two. This is "one-and-a-half axes," and tools are the worked instance.

Two definitions must be kept rigorously apart, because conflating them is exactly v1's error.

- An **enabling second dimension** is an intrinsic property of the element, orthogonal to scope, that the *time*-lever reads in order to vary independently of scope. Content-type for files; lifecycle for agents. It is not itself an economizing lever; it does not decide slot-occupancy. It is the *thing the time-lever attaches to* so that the time decision is not forced to be the scope decision. You can have it and still have exactly two economizing axes.

- A **third economizing lever** would be a genuine *third coordinate of the provisioning*, a further independent decision about slot-occupancy beyond where and when. The framework predicts there is none of these for the window-as-characterized (argued, with the soft joint above). An enabling dimension is emphatically *not* one of these; it is a feature of the element, not a coordinate of the call.

The distinction is the keystone. An enabling second dimension is what *lets the two axes be two*; a third economizing lever would be what *makes the two axes three*. Files have the former (content-type) and not the latter, which is why they get a clean two-axis grid and not a three-axis one. The next section makes this the centerpiece of the file case, and it is where v1 went wrong.

## File instances: files factor cleanly *because* they carry content-type

*Context as Architecture* economizes the population of **files** (content placed before the model). It resolves file-positioning into two independent axes and shows the two together decompose the full "what should this agent see?" question. Map them:

- **Scope axis = the routing hierarchy (L0 / L1 / L2).** This is the *where*. A file's routing level partitions the situations in which it is in-scope: an L0 root file is in-scope everywhere (global identity); an L1 workspace file is in-scope only within that workspace; an L2 task rule is in-scope only for a matched task. That is scope exactly: route a file so it is present only where it is relevant. The companion paper's "vertical axis: determines where the agent operates and what scope the loading rules apply to" is the scope axis of this root, named for files.

- **Time axis = the load pattern (always / on-demand / per-item).** This is the *when*. A file's load pattern schedules its presence within the situations where it is in-scope: identity and situational context are *always* loaded; references load *on demand* by task type; working files load *per work-item*. That is time exactly: present a file only when, and for as long as, it is relevant. The companion paper's "horizontal axis: decides what the agent loads, when" is the time axis of this root, named for files.

Now the correction, because it is the keystone reframe of this whole paper, and v1 got it backwards.

The companion paper does not call its horizontal axis "load pattern." It calls it **"load pattern combined with content type,"** and it is emphatic that content-type is its own independent thing: *"When something gets loaded and what kind of information it is are independent questions; the architecture answers both."* The four content-types, identity, situational context, reference material, working files, are a real classification, orthogonal to scope and orthogonal to the load-timing. Downstream of the paper, the ICM SPEC's file linter is even sharper: it runs `classify(file_path, tree, claude_md) -> {routing_level, content_type, load_pattern}`, **three** independent, checkable, violable attributes, and lints them against named rules. Content-type is not a stray label; it is a first-class attribute a tool *checks* and a file can *violate*.

**v1 waved this away as "a descriptive tag riding the time axis."** That was wrong, and it was wrong in a way that contradicted the paper's own source. Worse, v1 treated the file population's third checkable attribute as an *embarrassment* to be explained away (a "smoking gun" against "exactly two economizing axes"). The refined thesis flips this completely: **content-type is not a problem for the two-axis claim; it is the *reason* the two-axis claim holds for files.** Here is the argument.

Content-type is the file population's **enabling second dimension** in the precise sense defined above. It is an intrinsic property of a file, orthogonal to scope, that the *time*-lever (load pattern) reads in order to vary independently of scope. *Why* is an identity file always-loaded? Because it is identity. *Why* is a reference on-demand? Because it is reference, used to make things, loaded when the making calls for it. *Why* is a working file per-item? Because it is a product, created and consumed in one work-item. The load pattern is *keyed to content-type*, and content-type is *not scope*. That is exactly what buys the independence the previous section demanded: a file's *when* can move while its *where* stays pinned, because the *when* tracks the *content-type*, not the scope. The fractal property the companion paper observes, the load pattern repeating unchanged at every routing level, is precisely the statement that the time axis is free at every value of scope, which is only possible because the property keying the time axis (content-type) is orthogonal to scope.

So the rigorous statement is: **files have *three* independent checkable attributes (routing-level, content-type, load-pattern) but *two* economizing axes (scope = routing-level; time = load-pattern), and the third attribute, content-type, is the enabling dimension that makes the two economizing axes genuinely independent rather than collapsed.** Content-type does not decide slot-occupancy; routing-level and load-pattern do. It is checkable and violable (a file mis-typed as identity when it is really a work product is a real, lintable error) precisely because it is a real attribute, and it is *not* a third economizing lever precisely because the slot-occupancy decision still factors into where and when. The SPEC checks three attributes; the window is economized on two axes; the third attribute is what holds the two apart. v1's "smoking gun" is, correctly read, the confirming evidence: files get a clean two-axis grid *because* they carry content-type, and this is exactly parallel to agents carrying lifecycle. The keystone is that an enabling-dimension and a third-economizing-lever are different things, and content-type is the first.

The companion paper's central result, that ICM's five layers are more clearly two independent axes, is, in this root's terms, the discovery that *files are economized on scope and time*, and the role content-type plays in making those two independent is the discovery, made here, of *why that decomposition is available for files at all.*

## Agent instances: agents factor cleanly, with an honest asymmetry

*The agent-role topology* economizes the population of **agents** (roles placed in the structure). It resolves role-positioning into two orthogonal axes and a grid. The mapping is real and the time axis is clean, but v1 oversold it ("literally identical / shared"), and the honest account has an asymmetry worth stating plainly, because the asymmetry is itself evidence for the refined thesis.

- **Time axis = lifecycle (standing vs ephemeral).** This is the *when*, and it is the **cleaner cross-population invariant**, the part that ports across populations with no fudging. A role's lifecycle schedules its presence in time: a *standing* role persists, wakes on a schedule, and survives between runs (the role-population analogue of always/on-cadence loading); an *ephemeral* role is invoked per task, authors its product, and terminates (the analogue of per-item loading). Lifecycle is the agent population's **enabling second dimension**: an intrinsic property of a role, orthogonal to its position-in-the-structure, that the time-lever reads so that *when* a role is present varies independently of *where* in the authority structure it sits. That is exactly content-type's job in the file population, played by lifecycle here. It is *why* agents, like files, get a clean two-axis grid: the time setting is keyed to a property (lifecycle) that is not the scope-like first axis.

- **First axis = Position (Lead vs Executor), which is scope-*like*, not literal scope.** Here is the asymmetry v1 hid. A role's **Position**, does it hold a board and delegate, or author a product, is a *functional role*: it says *where in the authority structure* the role operates. That is scope-*like*, it routes a role to where it is relevant in the org, away from where it is not, and the companion paper does treat it as the "where" of the role grid. But it is **not a literal partition of situation-space** the way a file's routing level is. A file's L0/L1/L2 literally partitions the *situations the model can be in* and admits the file only in its partition; that is scope in the same sense the principle uses. Position partitions *authority roles*, not situations. It is analogous to scope, and it functions as the role grid's first axis, but calling it "the same axis as file scope" would overstate the case, and v1 did.

What *is* genuinely shared, and this is the honest residue of v1's "shared" claim, is **the routing hierarchy itself, as the level property.** A Lead carries a `level` (L0 / stream / engagement) that runs along the *same routing hierarchy* the file layer's scope axis uses; the topology paper says so directly: *"Context as Architecture made L0/L1/L2 a property of the vertical routing axis, not three kinds of file, and the role layer mirrors that exactly. One routing hierarchy, read once for files and once for roles."* So the genuinely shared object is the routing hierarchy read as a *level property*, not the whole first axis. The agent's first axis is Position (Lead/Executor), which *carries* a level property that is shared with files; it is not itself the file scope axis.

The downgrade, stated cleanly: the agent population is **analogous** to the file population, not identical to it. The routing hierarchy is genuinely shared as a *property* (level). The **time axis (lifecycle) is the cleaner cross-population invariant**, mapping onto the file load pattern with no asymmetry. The first axis is scope-*like* (Position, a functional role) rather than literal scope. Being honest about this asymmetry is not a retreat; it is evidence for the thesis. Both populations get a clean two-axis grid, and in both the *reason* is an intrinsic second dimension keying the time-lever (content-type for files, lifecycle for agents). The time axis ports perfectly; the scope axis ports as an analogy. That the *enabling-dimension* story holds identically across both, while the scope story is only analogous, is precisely what the refined thesis predicts: the clean form is purchased by the second dimension, and that purchase is the invariant.

### The empty cell, predicted

The mapping predicts a feature of the topology that the topology paper had to discover separately: its **empty cell**. The (Lead, Ephemeral) cell is provably empty because "holding a board requires persistence." In this root's terms: a Lead's board is a *standing* element by construction, so its time setting is pinned. The empty cell is the time axis being *constrained by* a first-axis value, a Lead's role (hold a persistent board) forces its lifecycle (standing), leaving the ephemeral lifecycle for that role uninhabitable. This is a *constraint between two genuinely independent axes*, not a collapse of two into one: lifecycle still runs free among Executors, so the axis is real; it is merely pinned at one first-axis value. **An empty-cell theorem is a thing only a real two-axis grid can have**, you need two independent coordinates for one combination of them to be provably uninhabitable, which is exactly why tools, which do not have two independent axes, cannot reproduce it (next section). The root predicts that wherever a population has an enabling second dimension *and* one first-axis value structurally demands a particular time setting, the opposite time-value for that first-axis value is an empty cell. The topology's theorem is a corollary.

### Two populations, the shape side by side

| | First axis (where / where-like) | Time axis (when) | Enabling 2nd dimension (keys the time axis) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Files** (*Context as Architecture*) | routing hierarchy: L0 / L1 / L2 (literal scope) | load pattern: always / on-demand / per-item | **content-type**: identity / situational / reference / working |
| **Agents** (*agent-role topology*) | Position: Lead / Executor (scope-*like*; carries the shared level property) | lifecycle: standing / ephemeral | **lifecycle** *is* the second dimension that keys the time axis |

A note on the agent row's last two columns: for agents, lifecycle plays *both* the time-axis role and the enabling-second-dimension role, because the property the time-lever reads (standing-vs-ephemeral) is the very property that *is* the schedule. For files the two are distinct columns (content-type keys the load pattern but is not itself the load pattern). This is a small structural difference between the populations, not a problem; what matters is that in both, the time axis is free of the first axis *because* an intrinsic property orthogonal to the first axis is available to key it. The routing hierarchy is genuinely shared (the literal scope of files, the level property of agent Leads). The time column ports cleanly. The first axis ports as analogy. Two populations, one economy, one condition met twice.

## Tools: the worked bounding case (one-and-a-half axes)

The strongest test of the refined thesis is not a third clean confirmation; it is a population that meets the *universal* economy but *fails the condition* for the clean two-axis form. Tools are that population, and working them through is the demonstration that the condition is real and not decorative. This is kept general and illustrative, not tied to one personal workspace.

A tool (a function-definition placed before the model: its name, description, parameter schema, examples) occupies window slots and competes for attention exactly as files and agents do. So it is economized, and the *universal* economy applies in full.

**Tools obey the SCOPE economy cleanly.** Route the schema. Some tools are always-present with their full schema (the handful the agent needs in nearly every situation). Some are present *by name only*, with the schema deferred until a fetch-or-search step actually pulls the full definition (the long tail: present enough that the agent knows the tool exists, absent enough that the schemas do not flood the window). Some are workspace-scoped: present only in the situations (repos, projects, channels) where that tool is wired. That is scope exactly, a partition of situations with the tool admitted only in its partition, and it is the dominant, load-bearing economization for tools: with hundreds of available tools and a finite window, *which schemas are present* is the whole game.

**But the TIME-lever collapses into scope, because a tool carries no intrinsic second dimension to hold them apart.** Consider deferring a tool's schema until a search step surfaces it. Is that a *when* decision or a *where* decision? It is both, inseparably, and that is the point. Deferring the schema *is* the act of scoping it out of the current provisioning and into a later, narrower one; "load this tool's schema only when a step calls for it" and "this tool's schema is in-scope only in the situations where a step calls for it" are *the same decision stated twice*. There is no property of the tool, analogous to content-type or lifecycle, that the time-lever could read to make "when is this schema present" vary independently of "in which situations is this schema present." A file's load pattern keys on content-type; an agent's presence keys on lifecycle; a tool's schema-presence keys on... nothing but the situation and whether this is the call that needs it. So the *when* has nothing to bind to except the *where*, and the two levers, which are genuinely two for files and agents, are one-and-a-half for tools: a full scope axis, plus a time-lever that exists (you really can defer a schema) but cannot stand free of scope because it has no second dimension to stand on.

**No empty-cell theorem reproduces, and that is the diagnostic.** For files and agents you can write a two-axis grid and find a provably uninhabitable cell (the empty (Lead, Ephemeral) cell). For tools, try to build the grid: one axis is scope (workspace-scoped / global), the other would be "time" (always-present / deferred), but the second axis is not independent of the first, deferring *is* a scoping, so the "grid" is not a grid of two free coordinates; it is one coordinate drawn twice. With no second free coordinate, there is no combination of two coordinates to prove uninhabitable, so no empty-cell theorem exists for tools. The *absence* of the theorem is not a gap in our analysis; it is the positive signature of a population that fails the condition. A clean grid hosts theorems; a one-and-a-half-axis economy cannot, and tools cannot.

This is the worked demonstration of the thesis's condition. Like the topology's empty cell, the **boundary case proves the rule**: the empty cell proved that lifecycle is genuinely independent of position (only independent axes have provably-empty combinations), and tools prove the converse, that *without* an intrinsic second dimension, the time-lever collapses into scope and no clean two-axis grid forms. Files and agents *confirm* the condition by meeting it; tools *bound* it by failing it. The thesis needed exactly this third population to be more than an observation about two papers that happened to rhyme. A tool *could* be given an enabling second dimension by some future scheme (if, say, tools acquired an intrinsic "kind" that keyed a genuine schedule independent of scope, the way content-type keys load pattern), and the thesis predicts that *if* such a dimension were introduced and were genuinely orthogonal to scope, tools would *then* factor into two peer axes and could host an empty-cell-style theorem. That conditional is itself a falsifiable prediction (below).

## The prediction, and what would falsify it

A framework that only re-describes what is already known earns little. The payoff of stating the principle as a principle, with a condition, is that it *predicts*, and a prediction is only worth the conditions that would kill it. The refined thesis predicts more sharply than v1's did, because it predicts not just *that* a population is economized by where and when (the universal part, hard to falsify because hard to escape) but *whether* a population will factor into two clean peer axes (the conditional part, sharply falsifiable).

### The prediction

**Universal:** any population of elements placed before a finite, all-at-once, per-call window is economized by where (scope) and when (time). **Conditional and sharper:** a population factors into two independent peer axes, with a clean grid and the possibility of empty-cell-style theorems, **if and only if** it carries an intrinsic second dimension, orthogonal to scope, for the time-lever to bind to. Files and agents have one and factor; tools lack one and do not. The forward predictions for the next populations a Bessemer-style treatment would tackle now come in two parts each, a where/when claim and a *does-it-have-an-enabling-dimension* claim:

- **Memories.** A memory entry occupies a slot when recalled. Universal claim: scope (which situations is this memory relevant to) by time (always-surfaced identity-like memory vs on-demand recall vs single-task scratch). Conditional claim: memories *do* appear to carry an intrinsic second dimension, a memory-*kind* (semantic / episodic / procedural, or identity-like vs scratch) orthogonal to which situation recalls it, so the prediction is that memories factor into two clean peer axes, like files, and could host an empty-cell theorem (e.g. a kind that structurally must be always-surfaced pins its time value).

- **Connections.** An integration's surface (its available actions, auth state, capabilities) occupies slots when present. Universal claim: scope (which workspaces is this connection wired into) by time (persistently mounted vs activated-on-demand). Conditional claim: this is the population to watch, because a connection may be *tool-like*, its "activate on demand" may collapse into "scope it to the situation that needs it," in which case connections join tools at one-and-a-half axes. The prediction is genuinely uncertain here, which is what makes it worth testing.

- **Skills.** A skill's instructions occupy slots when the skill is loaded. Universal claim: scope (which situations the skill applies to) by time (the progressive-disclosure load pattern, present only when triggered). Conditional claim: skill-level progressive disclosure is *already* a time-axis mechanism, so the test is whether a skill carries a second dimension keying that disclosure independently of scope, or whether, like a tool, its "disclose on demand" is just "scope to the triggering situation." Skills may land on either side of the condition, and saying so in advance is the prediction.

The strong, falsifiable form is never merely "you can describe it with where and when" (the universal part, which the tautology objection rightly notes is cheap). It is the *conditional*: **does this population have an intrinsic second dimension, and does its factoring track exactly whether it does?** That is the claim with teeth, and it is the claim tools already illustrate by failing in the predicted way.

### What would falsify it

The refined thesis is more falsifiable than v1's, because the strengthened falsifiers turn on the *condition*, which is a structural fact about a population that cannot be redescribed away by stretching "where and when." Any *one* of the following, demonstrated for a real population placed before a real context window, falsifies the thesis as stated:

1. **A clean two-axis factoring with NO enabling second dimension.** A population that lacks any intrinsic property orthogonal to scope for the time-lever to bind to, yet nonetheless factors into two genuinely independent peer axes (time free at fixed scope, with at least one combination provably uninhabitable, an empty-cell-style theorem). This directly contradicts the condition: the thesis says no enabling dimension means no clean factoring. It cannot be redescribed away, because "is there an intrinsic property orthogonal to scope keying the schedule" is a yes/no question about the population's own structure, not about how roomy "where and when" are. (Tools are the worked case the thesis already walks through, and they behave exactly as it predicts: no second dimension, no clean factoring, no theorem; an internal consistency check, not independent confirmation.)

2. **An enabling second dimension that does NOT yield a clean factoring.** A population that *does* carry an intrinsic, scope-orthogonal property keying its time-lever, yet *fails* to factor into two independent peer axes (time stays pinned to scope despite the available dimension). This contradicts the condition from the other side, the dimension is supposed to be sufficient for independence, and would show the condition is necessary-but-not-sufficient, breaking the "if and only if."

3. **A genuine third economizing lever.** An examined population whose established, downstream-checkable scheme has a *third* independently-checkable, violable attribute that genuinely *decides slot-occupancy*, a real third coordinate of the provisioning, not merely *labels* the element (content-type is checkable and violable but does *not* decide occupancy, so it is the enabling dimension, not a third lever, and does not falsify). If organizing some population demonstrably needs a third orthogonal *occupancy*-deciding lever to be complete, "two economizing axes" is dead. This is the criterion from the exhaustiveness section turned into a falsifier: find a property the criterion cannot file as scope, time, or input, and that genuinely conditions occupancy.

4. **The situation sink overflowing.** A demonstrated coordinate of a provisioning that the exhaustiveness criterion cannot cleanly file as scope (a stable feature of the world), time (a feature of the element's schedule), or input (a heuristic feeding the two), and that must be conditioned on for the occupancy decision. Because exhaustiveness is *argued, not proven*, with "situation" as an admitted elastic sink, this falsifier is genuinely reachable: it is the joint giving way. Finding it would not show the universal economy is wrong so much as show its *exhaustiveness* was over-argued, that there is a third coordinate "situation" was quietly absorbing.

5. **A window without the two structural properties.** A future architecture that relaxes boundedness (an effectively unbounded window where nothing competes, no scarcity, the principle is vacuous, not false, but no longer load-bearing) or relaxes all-at-once provisioning, or exposes a *third addressing coordinate* to the provisioning decision beyond situation and moment (which would admit a real third axis per the elimination). This bounds the claim's reach rather than refuting it as scoped, but honesty requires flagging it as the condition under which the principle stops applying rather than being wrong.

What does *not* falsify it: finding a population you *can* describe with scope and time (the universal part succeeding, *provided* the conditional claim about its factoring is also borne out); finding heuristics like priority or recency (inputs to the two levers, predicted, not counterexamples); finding compression or summarization (operates within the time setting, not beside it); finding a *checkable label* that does not decide occupancy (that is an enabling dimension or a descriptive tag, predicted by the framework, content-type being the worked example, not a third lever).

## Epistemic status

This matters enough to state cleanly and not bury, because the parts of this paper have different standing and conflating them would oversell the work. v1's failure was an altitude failure: it stated an argued framework with theorem-like confidence. This version keeps the two halves visibly at different altitudes.

**What is argued (framework), and how firmly.** That the context window is a bounded, per-call, all-at-once slot-set; that economizing it is universally a matter of where (scope) and when (time); that the economy factors into two independent *peer* axes exactly when the population carries an intrinsic second dimension keying the time-lever; and that the file and agent populations are exact instances meeting that condition (content-type, lifecycle) while tools are an instance failing it. The *universal* economy and the *condition* are argued by the structural reasoning in "The two levers" and "The condition," and demonstrated across three populations. **Within the framework, the parts have different firmness, and the paper wears the difference.** The *minimum* (one axis cannot economize a population whose levers separate) is the firmest. The *condition* (enabling dimension iff clean factoring) is well-supported: two populations confirm by meeting it, one bounds by failing it. **Exhaustiveness, that there is no *third* economizing lever, is the softest part of the framework half, and is argued, not proven**, because its key premise ("a provisioning is a (situation, moment) pair") leans on an elastic "situation" that can absorb candidate third coordinates. The exhaustiveness section names that joint, offers a criterion to distinguish a coordinate-of-a-provisioning from an input-to-the-decision, and concedes the criterion has a fuzzy edge. So the framework half is argued, and its softest sub-claim (exhaustiveness) carries its conjectural status as openly as the generalization half does, which is the explicit correction of v1's overclaim.

**What is conjectured (generalization).** That the same conditional structure, universal where/when economy, clean two-axis factoring iff an enabling second dimension, *recurs for populations not yet examined*: memories, connections, skills, and any future element. Three instances (files and agents confirming the clean form, tools bounding it) are consistent with it and considerably stronger evidence than v1's two, because the third instance tests the *condition* rather than merely re-confirming the pattern. But three instances do not establish a universal, and the paper does not pretend they do. The generalization is offered for refutation, with the strengthened falsifiers above, which turn on the structural yes/no of "does this population carry an enabling dimension" rather than on the elastic "can you describe it with where and when."

The line between the two halves is the line between "this is how the three populations fit under one economy with one condition, argued" and "this is what I bet the next population will show, conjectured." The first is defended; its softest joint (exhaustiveness) is flagged. The second is a wager with its losing conditions written down. This paper does *more* than v1 claimed to: it states the universal economy *and* the precise condition for its clean two-axis form, evidenced by three populations rather than asserted of all. That larger, conditional claim is more interesting and more falsifiable than the flat "exactly two, everywhere" v1 overreached for, and naming its altitude honestly is part of the house discipline both companion papers keep.

## Adjacent concepts

Where this principle sits relative to ideas it borrows from and should be told apart from.

**Context as Architecture (companion paper, this repository).** The first corollary, and the keystone instance: this principle applied to the population of files, which factors cleanly *because* files carry content-type. Its axes are this root's scope (routing hierarchy) and time (load pattern); its third checkable attribute (content-type) is this root's *enabling second dimension*, the property that lets the time axis run free of scope, not a third economizing lever and (contra v1) not a tag to be waved away. Its fractal property is the independence of the two axes made visible, which the enabling dimension is what underwrites.

**The agent-role topology (companion paper, this repository).** The second corollary: this principle applied to the population of agents, which factors cleanly *because* agents carry lifecycle. Its time axis (lifecycle) is this root's time axis and the cleaner cross-population invariant; its first axis (Position) is scope-*like*, a functional role, with the routing hierarchy genuinely shared as the level property. Its empty-cell theorem is a corollary of a first-axis value (Lead) pinning a time value (standing), and is the kind of theorem only a genuine two-axis grid can host. The topology's own closing conjecture is the seed this paper grows into a root, and sharpens with a condition.

**ICM (Van Clief and McDermott).** The grandparent methodology, reached through *Context as Architecture*. ICM's five layers are, per the companion paper, two axes; the ICM SPEC's file linter checks three independent attributes (routing-level, content-type, load-pattern), which is the concrete source of this paper's content-type-as-enabling-dimension argument: the SPEC's third *checkable* attribute is the file population's enabling second dimension, distinct from its two *economizing* axes. ICM is the lineage from which the file instance descends; this paper sits above it by abstracting the *resource* (the window) rather than the *population* (files).

**Caching and the working set.** The deepest technical analogy, and the one that motivates the "finite, all-at-once, re-provisioned per call" framing. A cache is a fixed-capacity slot-set refilled per access cycle; the working-set model (Denning) characterizes which pages must be resident for a process to run without thrashing. The context window is a working set for an inference call. Caching's two classic levers, *what to admit* (a placement/scope decision) and *what to evict / how long to keep* (a temporal/retention decision), are the scope and time axes in their systems-programming form. The "lost in the middle" effect is the window's version of cache pressure: the effective working set is smaller than nominal. This analogy is load-bearing, not decorative: it is *why* the two-lever economics is expected. (Note that even in caching the clean separation of admission from retention relies on lines carrying tags and reference-bits, a second dimension; a cache with no per-line metadata to schedule on would, like tools, collapse retention into admission. The analogy reaches the refined thesis too.)

**Rate-distortion and information theory.** The economizing intuition has an information-theoretic shadow: with a finite channel (the window's token budget) you cannot transmit everything, so you must choose what to send to minimize distortion (lost task-relevant information) under a rate constraint. Scope and time are the two degrees of freedom for spending a rate budget across situations and across moments. Offered as a lens, not a derivation; the paper claims only that the *shape* of the problem (finite channel, choose what to send) is the same, which is consistent with the two-lever conclusion.

**Progressive disclosure.** Norman, Tognazzini, and the UI tradition: surface what is needed now, hide what is not. This is the time axis for the human-attention resource; a single screen is a finite all-at-once slot-set for a person, refilled per interaction. The companion paper notes progressive disclosure as the principle of its load-pattern axis "applied against finite context windows." This root makes the connection structural: human attention and the context window are *both* finite all-at-once slot-sets, so both are economized scope-by-time. (And UI elements typically carry a content-kind, a second dimension, which is why disclosure can run free of placement, the enabling-dimension story ports here too.)

**Adjacent but distinct: prompt engineering and compression.** Both operate *within* the framework, not beside it. Prompt engineering optimizes the content of slots already allocated for one call; it is downstream of the scope-and-time decision about *which* elements get slots. Compression and summarization change *how much* of an element occupies its slot; they are implementations within a time setting ("present a smaller version, now"), not a third axis orthogonal to where and when. Naming them keeps the "two economizing axes" claim honest: neither is a counterexample, both are inside.

## Glossary

Terms specific to this paper, plus the foundational terms it relies on. Alphabetical.

**All-at-once attention.** The property that a model attends to its entire filled window simultaneously in one inference call, rather than reading it as a stream with forgetting. Together with boundedness, the property that forces a per-call slot-occupancy decision and makes a provisioning a (situation, moment) pair.

**Bounded context window.** The first structural property of the window: it holds a finite number of tokens, and (under degradation effects) attends well to even fewer. The source of the scarcity the principle economizes.

**Bounding case.** A population that meets the universal where/when economy but *fails* the condition for the clean two-axis form, demonstrating the condition is real. Tools are the worked bounding case: they obey scope cleanly but their time-lever collapses into scope for lack of an enabling second dimension, so no empty-cell theorem reproduces. The analogue, for the condition, of the topology's empty cell for independence: the boundary proves the rule.

**Content-type.** The file population's intrinsic second dimension: what *kind* of thing a file is (identity, situational context, reference, working file), orthogonal to scope and orthogonal to load-timing. A checkable, violable attribute in the ICM SPEC, distinct from the two economizing axes. The *enabling dimension* that keys a file's load pattern and thereby lets the time axis run free of scope. Not a third economizing lever (it does not decide slot-occupancy); the keystone distinction of this paper.

**Economization.** The act of deciding, for each candidate element, whether it occupies a window slot in a given provisioning. Reduces, with no residue (argued, not proven, see *exhaustiveness*), to a scope decision and a time decision.

**Element (placed before the model).** Any unit that can occupy window slots and thereby compete for finite attention: a file, an agent/role, a tool, a memory, a connection, a skill.

**Enabling second dimension.** An intrinsic property of an element, orthogonal to scope, that the *time*-lever reads so that the element's *when* can vary independently of its *where*. Content-type for files; lifecycle for agents. The condition for the clean two-axis form: a population factors into two independent peer axes exactly when it has one. *Not* an economizing lever, it does not decide slot-occupancy; it is what the time-lever binds to. Sharply distinct from a *third economizing lever*.

**Exhaustiveness.** The property that scope and time *together* fully economize the window, with no third *economizing* lever. **Argued, not proven**: the elimination rests on "a provisioning is a (situation, moment) pair," whose "situation" is an elastic sink that can absorb candidate third coordinates. The paper offers a criterion (a coordinate of a provisioning vs an input to the decision) and concedes its fuzzy edge. The softest part of the framework half.

**Independence (orthogonality).** The property that fixing one axis leaves the other free. *Earned, not automatic*: it requires an enabling second dimension for the time-lever to bind to. Present for files (via content-type) and agents (via lifecycle); absent for tools, whose time-lever collapses into scope. A pinned value at one axis-value (Lead forces standing) is a *constraint between independent axes*, not a loss of independence, which would require pinning everywhere.

**Lifecycle.** The agent population's time axis *and* its enabling second dimension: standing (persists, wakes on schedule) vs ephemeral (per-task, terminates). The cleaner cross-population invariant, mapping onto the file load pattern with no asymmetry. The property that lets an agent's presence-in-time vary independently of its Position.

**One-and-a-half axes.** The shape of a population that obeys the scope economy cleanly but whose time-lever collapses into scope for lack of an enabling second dimension: a full scope axis plus a time-lever that exists but cannot stand free of scope. Tools are the worked instance. Cannot host an empty-cell theorem.

**Population.** A class of elements all of the same kind placed before the window: files, agents, tools, memories, connections, skills. The universal economy applies to each; the clean two-axis form applies to those carrying an enabling second dimension.

**Position.** The agent population's first axis: Lead vs Executor (board-ownership). A *functional role* that is *scope-like* (where in the authority structure a role operates) but is *not* a literal partition of situation-space the way file scope is. Carries the shared level property (the routing hierarchy). The honest asymmetry: the agent time axis ports to files exactly; the agent first axis ports as an analogy.

**Provisioning.** One filling of the window for one inference call. Identified, *from the economizing decision's standpoint*, by the *situation* the system is in and the *moment* the call happens. That a provisioning has only these two coordinates is the argued (not proven) premise of exhaustiveness; "situation" is the elastic term flagged as a soft joint.

**Routing hierarchy.** The nested scopes L0 / L1 / L2. The file population's literal scope axis, and the agent population's *level property* (carried by the Lead Position). The one object genuinely shared between the two companion papers; v1's "literally shared first axis" was an overclaim, the shared thing is the hierarchy-as-level-property, not the whole first axis.

**Scope (lever / axis, "where").** The first lever: route an element so it occupies slots only in the situations where it is relevant. A *partition* of the space of situations. A genuine axis for every population examined; the dominant, load-bearing economization for tools.

**Slot-set.** The window seen as a fixed-capacity set of occupied-or-not positions, filled per call. The framing that places the window in the family of caches, working sets, and register files.

**Space-by-time economy.** The paper's name (from the topology paper's closing conjecture) for the universal claim that a finite, all-at-once, per-call resource is economized by space (scope) and time. Universal as an *economy*; clean as a *two-axis factoring* only under the condition (an enabling second dimension).

**Third economizing lever.** A hypothetical genuine third coordinate of a provisioning that decides slot-occupancy beyond where and when. The framework predicts none exists for the window-as-characterized (argued, soft joint at exhaustiveness). Sharply distinct from an *enabling second dimension*, which is a property of the element, not a coordinate of the call, and does not decide occupancy. Conflating the two was v1's keystone error.

**Time (lever / axis, "when").** The second lever: schedule an element so it occupies slots only when relevant, and only for as long as. A *schedule* over moments with a duration. A genuine peer axis only when an enabling second dimension keys it (files, agents); collapses into scope without one (tools).

**Working set.** Denning's model of the set of pages a process must keep resident to run without thrashing. The closest technical ancestor: the window is a working set for an inference call, and the working-set levers (what to admit, how long to retain) are scope and time in systems-programming form.
